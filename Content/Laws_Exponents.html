An exponent refers to the number of times multiplied by itself.

 

For example, 5 x 5 x 5 is the same as 53. Here, five is multiplied by itself three times. Thus, 3 is an exponent, and 5 is a base.

     -You can read it as 'five to the power of three' or 'five to the 3rd power'.

Another example, 14 x 14 x 14 x 14 is the same as 144. Here, fourteen is multiplied by itself four times. Thus, 4 is an exponent, and 14 is a base.

     -You can read it as 'fourteen to the power of four' or 'fourteen to the 4th power'.

In this lesson, we will cover seven laws of exponents.

1) Product or Multiplication Rule:

    - When the bases are the same when multiplying, we can add their exponents.

Examples:

  • 23 x 25 = 28
  • 152 x 153 = 155
  • a3 x a10 = a13

Bonus:

  • 3a2 x 3a4 = 32a6      The base of 3a2 is the same as 3a4. So, we added the exponents to get 32a6.
  • 3y2 x 4y5 = 12y7      Only the of y2 is the same as y5. So, we added the exponents of y to get y7 and multiplied 3 and 4 to get 12.

2) Quotient or Division rule:

    - It is the opposite of the Product rule. When the bases are the same when dividing, we can subtract their exponents.

Examples:

  • 46 ÷ 44 = 42
  • 106 ÷ 103 = 103
  • m9 ÷ m1 = m8

Bonus:

  • 56x3 ÷ 53x = 53x2      The base of 56x3 is the same as 53x. So, we added the exponents to get 53x2.
  • 3y5 ÷ 5y3 = 2y3÷ 5      Only the base of y5 is the same as y3. So, we subtracted the exponents of y to get y3 and divided 2 and 5 to get 2÷5.
