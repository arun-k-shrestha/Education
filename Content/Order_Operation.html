Order of Operations

Go Back

Operations mean adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing, etc. So, the order of operations is a set of rules to solve a mathematical expression in the correct order.

When we see a problem like this: 2 x 4 - 3, should we multiply 2 and 4 or subtract 3 from 4?

The right way is to multiply 2 and 4 and then subtract later:

    2 x 4 - 3
    = 8 - 3
    = 5

The order of operations is:

    Parentheses    ( ),
    Exponents    x2,
    Multiplication or Division    X or ÷,
    Addition or Subtraction    + or -

Remember these by the acronym PEMDAS. Parentheses will have higher preferences than exponents and exponents will have higher preferences than multiplication or division, and so on.

In the previous example, we multiplied first and then subtracted later because multiplication has higher order than subtraction.

Let's take another example:

    (2+3) + 4 x 2
    = 5 + 4 x 2      We solved everything that was inside the parenthesis
    = 5 + 8      We multiplied 4 and 2 to get 8
    = 13      We added 5 and 8 to get 13

Did you see the order of operations? Parenthesis was followed by multiplication and addition. We didn't work with exponents, division, or subtraction, because the equation didn't contain those.

Here's another example:

    52 x 2 ÷ (3 x 4 - 2)
    = 52 x 2 ÷ (12 - 2)      We multiplied 3 and 4 to get 12 inside the parenthesis
    = 52 x 2 ÷ 10      We subtracted 2 from 12 to get 10 inside the parenthesis
    = 25 x 2 ÷ 10      We worked with exponents- 5 to the power of 2 is 25
    = 50 ÷ 10      We multiplied 25 and 2 to get 50
    = 5      We divided 50 by 10 to get 5

Notice that we multiplied first and divided later even though multiplication and division get the same preference. If multiplication and division come side by side, we choose the one on the left. This applies to addition and subtraction.

Here are two examples:

1) Multiplying first because it is on the left of Division

    10 x 2 ÷ 5
    = 20 ÷ 5
    = 4

2) Dividing first because it is on the left of Multiplication

    10 ÷ 2 x 5
    = 5 x 5
    = 25

We can also solve different parts of a problem separated + or - at the same time. It is not necessary, but it can reduce the number of steps we take to solve the problems

Example:

    2 x 6 + 4 ÷ 2    We can multiply 2 and 6, and divide 4 by 2 at the same time.
    = 12 + 2
    = 14

One more example:

    12 ÷ 3 + 4 x 2 - 6 x 2     We can divide 12 by 3 and multiply 4 and 2, and multiply 6 and 2 at the same time.
    = 4 + 8 - 12
    = 12 - 12
    = 0
